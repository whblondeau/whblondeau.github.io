What kind of situation am I looking for?

  • Flexible schedule is better than nailed-down. This operates in three senses. First, if I'm in the zone on a problem, I like to pursue it. Second, my days can get externally logistical, and I want the freedom to take care of business at need.

    Third, prolonged butt in chair can lead to mental dullness; when that happens, movement and fresh air are the best fixes. There are times when the most productive thing I can do is climb on my bicycle and go around the lake or something.

  • I like, and do well with, remote work. It dovetails naturally with my need for flexibility, and to incorporate physical exercise into my day. Given GitHub (or similar), SSH access to back-end servers, chat channels, video conferencing, and shared cloud documents, I've got a good track record.

    Do I therefore reject face to face collaboration? Hell, no. I'm fairly social, and I like sharing space with workmates.

  • I deliver more when I'm being pulled rather than pushed. As a rule, deadlines complicate my workplan and slow me down. On the other hand, when I'm completing tasks methodically, that's when I deliver best and quickest. (Anecdotes available on request. Also, see the item "Slow is smooth, smooth is fast" here.)

What type of technical work am I hoping to be doing? Basically, either something I'm already good with, or something that's unusually compelling.

  • Data Work. Whether design, analysis, setup, transformation, validation, maintenance, or a pleasant mixture of them all, data is my first love. It can be tabular, relational, hierarchical, or graph-based; it can be persisted in databases, filesystems; it can be modeled as XML, JSON, CSV or what have you.

    I'm very good with XML. (XPath, XSLT, XML Schema, XQuery.) I'm good with JSON and am in fact working on a personal project to usefully provide XPath-like declarative capability to the JSON world.

    My command of SQL is very good. I've worked with most of the open and proprietary relational databases. I know how to design schemas and use them. I'm comfortable and experienced with NoSQL databases (Redis particularly). I understand graph theory and various triple notations, and have no difficulty understanding and using graph databases (e.g. Neo4j). I have architected data warehouses, designed star schemas, defined data intake processes, and analyzed the integrity of audit trails.

  • Programming. I tend to prefer the back end: data access, service API design and implementation, utility scripting, CLI work. My preferred language is Python; I am also competent with JavaScript (especially NodeJS), and Java. I am familiar with a grab bag of other languages and frameworks.

    I do have a solid grasp of RESTful and microservice styles of service design and management. I've done both.

    If I could work with geospatial coding, I'd like to. And I'd very much like to have a chance to work at a Functional programming or Lisp shop. ::cough::Clojure::cough::

  • Technical Writing / Documentation. I'm good at this. I know how to do it, and why. I treat technical documentation seriously. I have a portfolio of work available.

    I also realize that the worth of this discipline is seldom well recognized, hence doesn't pay well. But I would welcome a chance at a properly compensated position.

  • DevOps. Over a year's recent experience working on Rabble's servers—under the tutelage of an expert of long standing—has buffed up my competence with the Linux command line, and my understanding of Linux systems administration, considerably.

    This, combined with my ability to write Python scripts (Python is ideal for scripting critical tasks that would be unwieldy for bash), and my long understanding of database operations, version control systems, and API services, qualifies me for a DevOps position.

    On Linux only, though. My understanding of Windows system administration is very sketchy.
